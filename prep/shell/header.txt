<p>The <b>walrus</b> (<i><b>Odobenus rosmarus</b></i>) is a large <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">pinniped</a> <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammal</a> with discontinuous distribution about the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> seas of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. It is the only extant species in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Odobenidae" title="Odobenidae">Odobenidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i>Odobenus</i>. This species is subdivided into two <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-msw3_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the Atlantic walrus (<i>O. r. rosmarus</i>), which lives in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, and the Pacific walrus (<i>O. r. divergens</i>), which lives in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. </p><p>Adult walrus are characterised by prominent <a href="/wiki/Tusk" title="Tusk">tusks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whiskers" title="Whiskers">whiskers</a>, and considerable bulk: adult males in the Pacific can weigh more than 2,000 kilograms (4,400 pounds)<sup id="cite_ref-Walrus:_Physical_Characteristics_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walrus:_Physical_Characteristics-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and, among <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">pinnipeds</a>, are exceeded in size only by the two species of <a href="/wiki/Elephant_seal" title="Elephant seal">elephant seals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fay85_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fay85-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Walrus live mostly in shallow waters above the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" class="mw-redirect" title="Continental shelves">continental shelves</a>, spending significant amounts of their lives on the <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> looking for <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bivalvia" title="Bivalvia">bivalve</a> molluscs. Walruses are relatively long-lived, social animals, and are considered to be a "<a href="/wiki/Keystone_species" title="Keystone species">keystone species</a>" in the Arctic marine regions. </p><p>The walrus has played a prominent role in the cultures of many <a href="/wiki/Circumpolar_peoples" title="Circumpolar peoples">indigenous Arctic peoples</a>, who have hunted it for meat, fat, skin, tusks, and bone. During the 19th century and the early 20th century, walrus were widely hunted for their <a href="/wiki/Blubber" title="Blubber">blubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walrus_ivory" title="Walrus ivory">walrus ivory</a>, and meat. The population of walruses dropped rapidly all around the Arctic region. It has rebounded somewhat since, though the populations of Atlantic and Laptev walruses remain fragmented and at low levels compared with the time before human interference. </p> <meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
